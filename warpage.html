Definition — ASIC
An Application-Specific Integrated Circuit (ASIC) is a semiconductor chip designed for one specific, dedicated purpose rather than general-purpose use. Unlike CPUs that run diverse software, ASICs are customized to maximize speed, power efficiency, and physical space for a single application — at the cost of flexibility.

Because ASICs are engineered for peak performance in a single task, they push the physical limits of semiconductor packaging far beyond standard off-the-shelf chips. A modern AI inference ASIC may dissipate 2,500 to 5,000 Watts of heat while integrating multiple processor dies and HBM memory stacks onto a single organic or glass substrate.

That level of thermal and mechanical stress makes ASIC package characterization non-negotiable. Warpage, delamination, and interconnect failures that go undetected before market release become catastrophic field failures. Our role is to find them first — before your ASIC ships.

Why ASIC Packages Are Warpage-Critical
ASICs are designed to do one thing at maximum efficiency — which means they run hotter, denser, and harder than any general-purpose chip. The organic substrate (ABF) has a CTE of ~15 ppm/°C while the silicon ASIC die is ~2.6 ppm/°C. Every thermal cycle bends, twists, and stresses the package. When the substrate reaches its glass transition temperature (Tg) it loses rigidity, dramatically increasing vertical expansion — accelerating warpage failure. ASIC Package — Thermal Stress Deformation

Warpage in PCBs carrying chips subjected to extreme thermal cycles is a structural deformation caused by the difference in the coefficients of thermal expansion (CTE) between the silicon of the component and the substrate materials. When the chip heats or cools, the board and component expand at different rates — generating massive mechanical stress on the solder balls and the internal vias of the PCB.

The organic substrate (ABF — Ajinomoto Build-up Film) holding the ASIC together has a CTE of ~15 ppm/°C while the silicon ASIC die sits at ~2.6 ppm/°C. At high temperatures, the material may exceed its glass transition temperature (Tg), losing rigidity and increasing its vertical expansion. At cryogenic temperatures, materials become brittle and prone to contraction-induced fractures.

To mitigate this effect, advanced substrates such as polyimide or low-CTE laminates are used, along with techniques such as symmetrical copper balance in the internal layers and the use of fill resins — called "underfill" — which help distribute mechanical stress away from critical electrical connections. A design that does not account for this thermal dynamic will inevitably result in solder fatigue failures or PCB delamination.

In large flip-chip BGA ASIC packages, warpage can exceed 300µm (12 mil) — well beyond JEDEC's 200µm tolerance limit. All ASIC packages — organic or glass — must be fully characterized before market release.

Our Methodology

Three-Phase ASIC
Characterization Protocol

01
Phase One

ASIC Extreme Environment Stress Testing

Before any optical or acoustic inspection, we subject the ASIC package to the harshest conditions it will realistically face — and beyond. The goal is to induce the maximum possible mechanical and thermal damage in a controlled environment, replicating full board-assembly soldering and years of field operation in accelerated time.

Thermal Cycling
Repeated exposure between extreme cold and heat (-40°C to +150°C per IPC-9701 / JEDEC standards). Simulates the heating and cooling the PCB will experience during manufacturing and field use — showing how the package deforms under differential expansion, helping identify materials or designs that may generate excessive warpage.
Reflow Simulation
The reflow oven is used to solder electronic components onto the PCB through a controlled thermal profile in which solder paste melts to establish electrical connections. PCBs tend to bow due to thermal expansion of materials — measuring deformation before and after reflow allows characterizing and minimizing warpage problems.
High-Temp Soak
Sustained dwell at operating temperature to evaluate creep, viscoelastic relaxation, and long-term ASIC package stability under conditions mirroring active AI accelerator and data center deployment environments.
Thermal Cycling & Reflow Simulation Equipment
02
Phase Two

ASIC Planarity and Structural Analysis

After stress conditioning, we deploy full-field non-contact optical measurement and acoustic microscopy to build a complete picture of the ASIC package's physical state — both surface planarity and internal structural integrity down to the die-substrate interface.

Shadow Moire
Full-field non-contact optical technique per JEDEC JESD22-B112. Creates interference fringe contour maps of out-of-plane displacement across the entire ASIC package surface in real time, including at reflow temperatures up to 260°C.
CSAM Acoustic Analysis
C-Mode Scanning Acoustic Microscopy uses high-frequency ultrasound (15–230 MHz) to image the ASIC interior layer by layer — detecting delamination, voids, cracks, and underfill separation invisible to any optical method.
FEM Correlation / DIC
Measured ASIC warpage and strain data correlated against Finite Element Models. Digital Image Correlation captures full-field displacement to validate simulation models for next-generation ASIC package design.
